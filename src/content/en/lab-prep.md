After completing the common core, the ideal skill profile isn't "knowing a little about every direction," but a **T-shape**: broad enough to read models, data, and experiments across the field, and deep enough to independently complete modeling or analysis in one specific problem.

### 9.1 The most persuasive portfolio

1. A mechanistic modeling project: equations, analytical/numerical checks, parameter sensitivity.
2. A real-data project: QC, leakage-free evaluation, uncertainty, and limitations.
3. A reproduction of a paper's key result: documented environment, discrepancies, and failed attempts.
4. A 2–4 page research note or technical blog post, plus a 10-minute oral presentation.

Two highly polished, rerunnable projects usually demonstrate more ability than ten projects that are nothing but notebook screenshots.

### 9.2 Questions to ask when choosing a lab

- What is the lab's core question: cellular mechanisms, systems-level representation, behavioral computation, clinical applications, or NeuroAI?
- Is the data self-collected or open data? Will you have exposure to experimental design and acquisition constraints?
- Is the methodology aimed at prediction, explanation, or intervention?
- Is there code review, data management, statistical consulting, and interdisciplinary co-mentorship?
- What data, compute, and ethical approvals would a student's first feasible project require?

### 9.3 Minimum competencies before entering research

- Can explain roughly 10 models from a core textbook in your own words, not just recite their names.
- Can reproduce a baseline from a paper's methods section, and pinpoint the source of any discrepancy.
- Can perform QC, correct data splitting, basic statistics, and visualization on real data.
- Can pose the question "what data would refute this explanation," and turn it into an analysis or experimental proposal.
- Can collaborate using Git, issues, environment files, data manifests, and code review.
