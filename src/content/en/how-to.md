This compilation borrows its organizing logic from the [CS Self-Study Guide](https://csdiy.wiki/): rather than just listing courses, it provides prerequisite dependencies, core courses, companion textbooks, assignments/projects, completion criteria, advanced branches, and self-study advice. It is an executable "training plan," not an encyclopedia of resources.

### 0.1 Tags

- **[core]**: should be completed if your goal is to enter research.
- **[recommended]**: strongly suggested, but can be substituted or compressed depending on your background.
- **[elective]**: only needed for the corresponding research direction.
- **[open]**: the main teaching materials are freely accessible online; live cohorts, certificates, or print books may be paid.
- **[platform]**: audit, assignment, and certificate policies on platforms like Coursera/edX change over time; defer to what the page says on the day you visit.
- **[book]**: usually requires purchase or library access, unless otherwise noted as having an author-provided open version.

### 0.2 The most important principles for using this roadmap

1. At each stage, pick only **1 main course + 1 reference book + 1 project**. Do not "binge" all resources at once.
2. At least half of your study time should go into derivations, coding, assignments, and interpreting results; watching videos is not a learning outcome.
3. Always split questions into three categories: **description/prediction (what)**, **mechanism (how)**, and **normative explanation (why)**. Accurate prediction does not mean you've found the biological mechanism.
4. Every model needs a baseline, train/test separation, uncertainty quantification, failure conditions, and a biological interpretation.
5. Produce a public, reproducible small project every 8–12 weeks, rather than waiting to "finish everything" before starting research.

### 0.3 If you only want the simplest main line

Completing the following four items in order is already a high-quality entry path:

1. [Neuromatch prerequisite materials](https://compneuro.neuromatch.io/prereqs/ComputationalNeuroscience.html): fill gaps in Python, linear algebra, calculus/ODEs, statistics, and basic neuroscience.
2. [Neuromatch Computational Neuroscience course book](https://compneuro.neuromatch.io/): build a panoramic view of model fitting, GLM, dimensionality reduction, dynamical systems, Bayes, hidden states, reinforcement learning, and causality.
3. [EPFL's *Neuronal Dynamics*](https://neuronaldynamics.epfl.ch/online/): deepen your mechanistic understanding of single neurons, spike statistics, networks, and cognitive dynamics, and complete the companion Python exercises.
4. Use [Allen Visual Coding Neuropixels](https://allensdk.readthedocs.io/en/stable/visual_coding_neuropixels.html), [IBL](https://www.internationalbrainlab.com/data), or [DANDI](https://docs.dandiarchive.org/) to build an open-data project, and then reproduce one key figure from a paper.
